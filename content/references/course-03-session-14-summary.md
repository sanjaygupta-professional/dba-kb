# Emerging Digital Technologies — Session 14: Group Presentations — Emerging Technologies in Industry Domains

**Instructor**: [[dr-sumitra-padman|Dr. Sumitra Padman]]
**Date**: 2026 (Session 14 of the Emerging Digital Technologies course, Cohort 8)
**Course**: [[course-03-overview-emerging-digital-technologies|Emerging Digital Technologies]] (Course 03)

Session 14 is the first of two assessed group-presentation sessions for Course 03. Five student groups — drawn from a globally dispersed cohort spanning India, UAE, Canada, Vietnam, Serbia, and Ireland — presented applied technology frameworks they had developed as group assignments. Each presentation was required to identify a real-world problem domain, map one or more emerging technologies to that domain, and propose a deployment framework with ROI and governance considerations. Dr. Sumitra Padman opened the session with a short framing discussion on the philosophy of the course itself: that understanding how technologies work, and when they are and are not appropriate, is more important for DBA-level leaders than learning to build them. She specifically cautioned against "fitting agentic AI" into problems that a simple ML classifier could handle more cheaply.

The session ran nearly four hours in total and covered five full presentations with Q&A. The recurring cross-cutting themes emerging from instructor feedback across all presentations were: (1) map technology applications specifically to the domain rather than stating them generically; (2) include an integrated framework diagram showing which technology sits where; (3) take the work beyond an academic exercise by grounding claims in quantified ROI and commercial viability; and (4) treat people, governance, and phased adoption as equally important as the technology itself. Dr. Padman encouraged every group to consider refining their assignments into publishable blog posts, journal articles, or even book chapters for the GGU journals.

The pre-session discussion also surfaced the broader curriculum design rationale: Course 03 was deliberately created because prior Applied AI Innovation courses repeated basics without touching genuinely new technologies (neuromorphic computing, digital twins, quantum computing, extended reality). The course is designed to be dynamic — updated each cohort as new technologies mature — with the long-term vision of master classes to backfill knowledge for alumni in older cohorts as technology evolves.

## Key Concepts

- **Cyber Resilience in Connected Healthcare (Group 4)** — Connected healthcare integrates IoT devices, wearables, smart monitors, and patient data into a unified ecosystem aimed at precision care and personalised treatment. This dramatically increases the attack surface. In 2024, the healthcare sector surpassed finance as the most frequently attacked sector, with the average cost per incident reaching approximately $10 million (citing WannaCry and Change Healthcare events). The group identified four root causes of vulnerability: the human factor (95% of incidents trace to staff behaviour), weak governance and compliance (57% of organisations admitting inadequate resources to meet HIPAA, GDPR, and EU AI Act requirements), IoT vulnerability management failures (poor device patching), and gaps in training framework alignment. Their proposed solution centred on three "game-changer" technologies: AI for proactive defence, blockchain for tamper-proof data integrity and supply chain trust, and digital twins for pre-emptive attack simulation.

- **AI-Driven Cyber Defence in Healthcare** — The group mapped AI to five specific cyber-security functions within healthcare. First, vulnerability management platforms such as Claroty and Armis analyse firmware and network behaviour, flag outdated or misconfigured settings, and assign risk scores to devices. Second, Security Orchestration Automation and Response (SOAR) systems monitor in real time, automatically isolating compromised accounts within seconds of a phishing event. Third, fine-tuned LLMs can parse and summarise complex regulatory requirements (HIPAA, FDA, EU AI Act) and check whether deployed systems are compliant. Fourth, GenAI-powered platforms can automatically compile audit-ready documentation from logs and evidence. Fifth, ML-powered personalised training platforms (such as KnowBe4 and CrowdStrike) send deliberate phishing simulations to employees, assess behavioural responses, and serve role-specific training tailored to each individual's demonstrated vulnerabilities.

- **Blockchain for Healthcare Data Integrity and Supply Chain** — Blockchain addresses two separate healthcare security problems simultaneously. For data integrity, a decentralised ledger creates immutable, tamper-proof audit trails of patient records and system activities, removing single points of failure. Smart contracts dynamically control access to patient data, giving patients ownership over their own health information while ensuring transactional accountability across insurers, providers, and device manufacturers. For supply chain security, blockchain can verify the provenance and authenticity of medical devices and pharmaceuticals at every step of the supply chain, eliminating counterfeit risk. The group noted that blockchain is conspicuously absent from most published healthcare use-case diagrams — representing a gap and an original contribution opportunity.

- **Digital Twins as Cyber Security Simulators** — Digital twins create virtual replicas of network infrastructure that can be used to run "what-if" analyses — simulating ransomware attacks, system failures, patching rollouts, and new software deployments — without touching live patient care systems. Key applications include: vulnerability discovery (identifying weaknesses before attackers do), advanced threat simulation (modelling malware and ransomware behaviour in a safe environment), incident response rehearsal (building and testing response playbooks), and dependency mapping (visualising cascade failure risks across interconnected systems). The twin enables a shift from reactive cybersecurity to precision readiness.

- **IoT in Smart Agriculture (Group 6)** — The global food system faces a structural crisis: by 2050 food production must rise 70% while arable land shrinks and water becomes scarcer. The group used a real case from a UAE-based agriculture consortium operating farms in Mauritania, the Middle East, and South Asia. Their five-pillar integration strategy for IoT adoption comprised: (1) identify primary outcomes (water reduction, disease loss reduction, yield increase); (2) define scope and crop prioritisation; (3) design connectivity and IoT layer (sensors, actuators, Raspberry Pi, Wi-Fi vs. LoRa); (4) run a pilot project before large-scale rollout; and (5) track KPIs (water saved, fertiliser reduction, labour hours, yield quality). IoT soil sensors demonstrated 30-40% irrigation reduction and 20-30% fertiliser savings in cited benchmarks. Financial modelling showed a five-year ROI breakeven within three years, with initial CapEx of ~€520k and benefits rising from €232k in year one to €359k by year four.

- **Ethical Governance in Smart Farming** — The group introduced a five-point ethical governance framework for agricultural IoT: (1) data rights and consent, with farmer-first data contracts specifying what is collected, why, how long, and who benefits commercially; (2) security and authentication (NIST and ETSI guidelines, no default passwords, incident response playbooks); (3) AI transparency and accountability (explainable recommendations with top-driver confidence scores and clear reasoning, farmer SLA policies for different conditions); (4) fair value sharing with farmers when their data is used for commercial insight (offering free replacements, insurance credits, and data access for model training); and (5) governance as a strategic enabler rather than a compliance cost. The group noted that GDPR compliance represents the global baseline, but local requirements (Singapore's PDPA, India-specific regulations) require additional localisation.

- **Micro-Clinic Model for Rural Healthcare in India (Group 9)** — India's rural healthcare divide is stark: 70% of the population lives in rural areas but 80% of doctors are concentrated in urban centres, and 82% of specialist posts at community health centres are vacant. The group proposed a "micro-clinic" model — small technology-enabled satellite clinics 5-10 minutes from rural villages, staffed by health technicians using portable diagnostic labs (weighing under 5 kg, capable of 60+ tests, battery-backed), connected via 4G/5G/Starlink satellite to urban doctors via video consultation. Patient vitals, symptoms, and test results are uploaded to a cloud platform that uses AI to flag anomalies, send alerts, and integrate with India's National Digital Health Mission through interoperability standards (FHIR) and Ayushman Bharat IDs. The commercial model showed CapEx of Rs. 4-6.4 lakh per clinic, monthly OpEx of Rs. 70k-1.1 lakh, potential revenue of Rs. 1.5-2.5 lakh/month, and break-even within 2-3 months (requiring 900-1,000 patient visits/month). Funding pathways identified: private/VC investment, CSR adoption by corporates, and government schemes (PMJAY integration).

- **Legacy Semiconductor Fab Automation (Group 7)** — Semiconductor legacy fabs (built in the 1990s) are semi-automated: operators manually scan wafer IDs, transport cassettes, inspect wafers visually, and log operations. In contrast, modern greenfield fabs are fully automated with overhead transport systems and no operators on the floor. The group proposed a layered automation framework using: IoT (NFC/RFID sensors replacing barcode scanning; microcontrollers such as Jetson Nano pushing data directly to Manufacturing Execution Systems); computer vision with Vision Language Models (VLMs) for automatic wafer alignment, laser scribe ID reading, and defect detection described in plain language; LLM-based orchestration layer acting as a natural-language interface to the MES ("what lots are waiting?"); robots and mobile carriers for lot transport; and digital twins for scheduling and capacity optimisation. The "see-reason-act" workflow describes the integrated agentic loop: VLM sees wafer images, LLM reasons over fab data, IoT/robotics act on instructions.

- **Digital Twins in Semiconductor Operations** — Digital twins in semiconductor fabs serve three distinct planning horizons. In scheduling and dispatching, the twin simulates which lot should go to which equipment and when, identifying bottlenecks before they manifest in the physical fab. In predictive maintenance, AI/ML models integrated with the twin predict equipment downtime and allow pre-emptive intervention. In ROI terms, even a 5% utilisation improvement translates to millions of dollars annually in a high-volume semiconductor environment. Companies such as Intel, Samsung, and GlobalFoundries have reportedly achieved 10-20% efficiency gains with AI automation. The group acknowledged that digital twins face slow adoption in this domain due to infrastructure costs, data complexity, and legacy system integration challenges. Dr. Padman suggested extended reality (XR) as a cost-effective intermediate step before full digital twin deployment, particularly for operator training outside the clean room.

- **Sustainable and ESG-Aligned Data Centres (Group 2)** — The explosive growth of AI is transforming data centre economics: AI clusters now require 100-300 megawatts per site, AI inference is far more energy-intensive than traditional search queries, and global data centre electricity demand is projected to reach 945 terawatt-hours by 2026. Traditional "fibre-first" site selection is giving way to "power-first" site selection — choosing locations based on grid access and renewable energy supply even at the cost of latency. The group proposed an ESG-integrated decision framework across six weighted pillars: business fit, power and grid structure, resiliency and security, ESG and compliance, economics (TCO), and operations and delivery. Key sustainability KPIs tracked were Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE, target below 1.4), Water Usage Effectiveness (WUE, target below 0.4), and Carbon Usage Effectiveness (CUE). Real-world cases cited: Dublin's grid operator pausing all data centre approvals due to Amazon, Google, and Microsoft collectively consuming 21% of Ireland's national electricity; Google's Finland data centre achieving 97% hourly matching with wind power; and Microsoft's partnership with Fortum to channel waste heat from data centres into district heating systems serving Finnish towns.

- **ESG Governance for Data Centres and the Role of Digital Twins** — The group argued that digital twins should be embedded from the design phase of a data centre — not just operationally — allowing customers to simulate energy flows, airflows, and heat distributions before ground is broken. Operationally, a "living twin" allows root-cause analysis of cooling and power failures in real time, enables change management rehearsal, and supports continuous training. The group also critiqued "greenwashing" (claiming annual carbon offsets that do not benefit the local community of the data centre), advocating for hourly carbon matching and real-time transparent reporting. EU taxonomy guidelines now mandate LEED and BREEAM certifications and enforce PUE, WUE, and waste heat reuse disclosures for data centres above 500 kW. The green data centre market is valued at $80 billion in 2025 and projected to reach $295 billion by 2035 (14% CAGR), with China (18.9% CAGR) and India (17.5% CAGR) leading growth.

- **Phased Adoption and the People Dimension** — A persistent cross-cutting theme across all five presentations was that technology adoption in complex domains (healthcare, agriculture, manufacturing, rural services) consistently underestimates the human and organisational change management challenge. Dr. Padman drew on her own 2010 experience proposing sensor-based agriculture to Gujarat farmers: farmers resisted because they did not understand the technology, not because the technology was wrong. The semiconductor group noted that legacy fab automation will displace 20-30% of operator roles, requiring deliberate workforce transition planning into higher-value roles (equipment maintenance, data analytics, AI supervision). The healthcare group framed the CISO's task as shifting from point solutions to integrated resilience: aligning people, processes, and technology together. Every group's recommendation section included "phased rollout" and "invest in people first" as primary success factors.

## Important Examples and Case Studies

**WannaCry and Change Healthcare Ransomware Attacks** — Used by Group 4 to quantify the stakes of healthcare cybersecurity. Each incident cost approximately $10 million on average and directly delayed patient care, making healthcare cyber-attacks categorically different from data breaches in other sectors: they have direct potential for patient fatalities.

**UAE Agriculture Consortium** — Group 6 used a real consortium operating farms in Mauritania, UAE, Europe, India, and Pakistan as their case. The consortium's pilot project in Serbia highlighted that farmer resistance to technology adoption is a cultural constant across geographies — Serbian farmers resisted just as strongly as Indian farmers in Dr. Padman's 2010 Gujarat pilot. The five-year financial model showed breakeven in year three (positive cash flow >€250k by year four), driven primarily by water savings, reduced chemical costs, and labour productivity gains.

**Dublin Data Centre Power Crisis** — Group 2 cited Dublin as a live case study in the limits of unmanaged data centre growth. Amazon, Google, and Microsoft collectively consumed 21% of Ireland's national electricity by 2024. Ireland's grid operator (EirGrid) responded by pausing all new data centre approvals until 2028. Developers are now exploring renewable energy zones in County Offaly, facing local community resistance — illustrating that power-first site selection must also account for social licence and community impact.

**Microsoft + Fortum Heat-as-a-Service (Finland)** — Microsoft partners with Finnish utility Fortum to channel waste heat from data centres into district heating networks serving multiple Finnish towns, contributing to national decarbonisation targets. This transforms what would be an energy waste stream into a revenue-generating service ("heat-as-a-service"), representing a model for data centre operators to shift from pure cost-efficiency to community value creation.

**Google 24x7 Carbon-Free Energy Matching** — Google's Finland data centre achieved 97% hourly matching with wind power. Other Google locations in the Netherlands and Iowa showed substantial but more variable hourly matching. Group 2 used this to argue that meaningful ESG reporting requires real-time, hourly carbon accounting — not annual offset purchases — and that the EU's Revised Energy Efficiency Directive (effective September 2024) is already mandating public disclosure of PUE, WUE, renewable share, and waste heat reuse for all EU data centres above 500 kW.

**Intel, Samsung, GlobalFoundries — Fab Automation ROI** — Group 7 cited internal and published research showing 10-20% efficiency gains already achieved by leading semiconductor manufacturers through AI automation. Their own modelling showed that attaching $100 IoT sensors to each of 1,000 tools in a legacy fab could deliver a 20% productivity boost by eliminating manual logging — equivalent to adding substantial production capacity at minimal capital cost.

**India Micro-Clinic: Patient Journey Scenario** — Group 9 walked through a concrete scenario: a rural farmer in a village on the Uttar Pradesh/Bihar border feels unwell. Rather than travelling 50-60 km to the nearest hospital (as the presenting member personally experienced), they walk 5-10 minutes to a micro-clinic. A health technician captures symptoms on a tablet, runs blood pressure and key diagnostics on a portable battery-backed device, uploads results to the cloud, and connects the patient via video to a doctor in Bangalore or Kolkata. The doctor prescribes digitally; the patient collects medication on-site. If the case is serious, the doctor refers to the nearest appropriate hospital with an available slot. Break-even at 900-1,000 patient visits per month.
