# Emerging Digital Technologies — Session 04: Cloud Computing, Edge Computing, and Cloud-Native Architecture

**Instructor**: Dr. Peturu Raj (Lead AI Engineer, Reliance Jio Platforms Limited)
**Date**: Session recorded and downloaded 2026-04-12
**Course**: Emerging Digital Technologies (Course 03)

Session 4 is anchored in infrastructure — specifically, the question of how enterprises store, process, and act on the massive volumes of data generated by the digital world. Dr. Peturu Raj opens by situating cloud computing within a five-pillar framework for digital business transformation (process excellence, architecture assimilation, infrastructure optimization, technology choice, and data leverage), then spends the bulk of the session unpacking infrastructure optimization. He traces cloud computing from its humble 1992 Amazon origin story through three distinct evolutionary eras — Cloud 1.0 (server virtualization), Cloud 2.0 (software-defined cloud), and Cloud 3.0 (cloud-native computing) — weaving in real Jio use cases throughout.

The second half of the session pivots to edge computing, which the instructor argues is the most consequential near-future development in the computing landscape. Rather than sending data thousands of kilometers to a central cloud, edge computing processes data locally and instantaneously — enabling real-time decisions that cloud latency makes impossible. Dr. Raj draws on Jio's own Raspberry Pi-based edge access-control system, and Jio's Bangalore airport CCTV-plus-AI project, to give concrete grounding to what can otherwise feel like abstract infrastructure concepts. He closes with a discussion of generative AI on cloud, the sustainability cost of LLM training, AI model compression techniques for edge deployment, and a brief look toward quantum computing as the next horizon beyond classical cloud and edge.

The session is interactive throughout. Students from insurance, banking, and technology backgrounds ask substantive questions about microservices dependency chains, multicloud strategy, private versus public cloud trade-offs, edge-versus-cloud decision criteria, and Moore's Law constraints on edge hardware. These exchanges push the instructor to give concrete analogies (Flipkart e-commerce, CAT exam server failure, Diwali load-spikes at Jio) that make the material accessible to non-technical participants.

## Key Concepts

- **Digital Transformation Technology Landscape** — Dr. Raj distinguishes between two classes of digital technologies: *digitization technologies* (IoT, edge), which convert physical and mechanical systems into digital systems that generate data; and *digitalization technologies* (cloud, AI, analytics), which convert that raw digital data into actionable insights and decisions. Cloud computing sits firmly in the second category. The interplay between the two is the engine of modern enterprise digital transformation.

- **The Data Explosion and the Analytics Gap** — Every day, 2.5 exabytes of data are generated globally — a volume equal to all data produced in the previous 50 years. Eighty-five percent of that data originates from machines (IoT sensors, devices, applications), not humans. Despite this, only 1–2% of the data generated daily is subjected to any meaningful analysis. The instructor argues this gap represents an enormous untapped opportunity, and that cloud infrastructure is the prerequisite for closing it.

- **Origin of Cloud Computing: Amazon's Elastic Computing Insight** — In 1992, Amazon was an online bookstore that experienced extreme seasonal demand spikes at Christmas. To handle the load, Amazon bought additional servers, only to find them sitting idle in January. Rather than accept the waste, Amazon began renting out those underutilized servers to third parties for a fee — a model originally called *elastic computing* that became the foundation of Amazon Web Services and the modern cloud industry. Cloud is therefore, at its core, *on-demand computing*, *online computing*, and *utility computing* (pay only for what you use, like electricity or water).

- **Virtualization and Resource Utilization** — Physical servers in a pre-cloud data center were, on average, only 15% utilized at any moment. The introduction of *hypervisors* (software that creates virtual machines on top of a host OS) allowed one physical machine to run multiple isolated operating systems simultaneously, raising effective utilization to 40–50%. This is the defining insight of Cloud 1.0: maximize the value of expensive compute hardware by subdividing it.

- **Containerization and Real-Time Scalability** — Containers are the next step beyond virtual machines. Where a VM provisions a full OS and takes 3–5 minutes to spin up, containers share the host OS kernel and start in seconds. This *real-time scalability* means cloud systems can auto-add compute capacity the instant demand spikes — preventing the kind of server failure that crashed the CAT exam portal when one lakh candidates logged in simultaneously. Kubernetes is the dominant container orchestration platform, holding approximately 85% of the market, with capabilities including self-scaling, self-healing, and declarative API-driven deployment.

- **Microservices Architecture** — Rather than building monolithic applications (where a change to any component requires taking the whole system offline), modern enterprise applications are composed of *microservices* — small, independently deployable, loosely coupled services that communicate via messaging (REST, GraphQL). The Flipkart e-commerce platform, for example, decomposes into distinct microservices for shopping cart, payments, analytics, and more. If the payments microservice needs an update, it can be brought down and redeployed without interrupting the shopping cart or analytics services. Multiple container instances can run each microservice simultaneously, providing built-in redundancy, fault isolation, and continuous delivery.

- **Non-Functional Requirements (NFRs) and Cloud** — Any enterprise application must satisfy not only *functional requirements* (what it does) but *non-functional requirements* (how well it does it). Any property ending in "-ility" qualifies: scalability, availability, reliability, accessibility, security, portability, resiliency. Cloud computing addresses NFRs structurally and automatically — adding virtual machines in minutes or containers in seconds in response to load, rather than requiring weeks-long hardware procurement cycles. The instructor uses the ATM machine as an intuitive NFR example: supporting multiple languages is an *accessibility* NFR; uptime guarantees are an *availability* NFR.

- **Cloud Evolution: Three Eras** — Dr. Raj structures cloud history as three generational leaps. *Cloud 1.0* virtualized compute servers only. *Cloud 2.0* (software-defined cloud) extended virtualization to storage and networking as well — giving rise to Software-Defined Networking (SDN), Software-Defined Storage, and Network Function Virtualization (NFV), which allows network functions like firewalls and load balancers to be configured in software rather than hardware. *Cloud 3.0* (cloud-native computing) integrates microservices, containers, Kubernetes orchestration, DevOps CI/CD pipelines, and Site Reliability Engineering (SRE) into a unified model that enables continuous, portable, resilient application delivery.

- **Public vs. Private vs. Hybrid Cloud** — A *public cloud* (AWS, Azure, GCP) is operated entirely by a third-party vendor; the enterprise has no direct visibility or control, and total cost of ownership (TCO) is lower. A *private cloud* (on-campus data center) gives the organization full control, full visibility, and higher data security (data never travels over public internet), but requires higher capital and operational expenditure. A *multicloud* or *hybrid cloud* approach — which Jio itself uses — distributes workloads across AWS, Azure, Google, and Jio's private cloud simultaneously, eliminating vendor lock-in and improving resilience. Migration between cloud providers remains technically non-trivial due to differing underlying operating systems (OpenStack, CloudStack, proprietary).

- **Cloud of Things (Cloud + IoT)** — IoT sensors and actuators are data generators; communication protocols (5G, Wi-Fi, LoRa) are data transmitters; and cloud is the data store and analytics engine. This three-layer model — generate, transmit, analyze — is the architecture of every modern IoT deployment. Cloud analytics platforms (Hadoop, Spark, Flink) and AI models running on cloud infrastructure convert raw IoT data streams into knowledge and actionable insights. Smart city examples discussed include cloud-alerted garbage collection vehicles and CCTV-based pedestrian monitoring systems.

- **Edge Computing: Real-Time Intelligence at the Source** — Edge computing moves data processing from a central cloud data center to the physical location where data is generated. The core motivation is latency: sending data from a Bangalore signal junction to a Mumbai cloud server and back takes approximately 14 milliseconds — enough time for a vehicle to have already crossed the junction. Edge processing happens in nanoseconds. A 2016 research paper first coined the term; by now, edge computing has become commercially critical for autonomous vehicles, robotic surgery, access control, and any other application where milliseconds matter. Edge devices are resource-constrained compared to cloud servers, but the gap is narrowing as device memory and compute power increase.

- **Edge AI (AI 2.0) and Model Compression** — Running AI models directly on edge devices — rather than sending data to the cloud for inference — is called *Edge AI* or *TinyAI*. The challenge is that modern LLMs consume terabytes or petabytes of memory, far beyond any edge device. Researchers (including Jio's team) use techniques such as *pruning*, *quantization*, and *knowledge distillation* to compress models by up to 80% — shrinking a 1 GB model to 100 MB or smaller — while retaining acceptable accuracy. Small Language Models (SLMs) that run on megabytes rather than gigabytes are an emerging class specifically designed for edge deployment.

- **Generative AI on Cloud and its Sustainability Cost** — Cloud infrastructure is the home for large language models in production. Training a single LLM requires hundreds of GPU servers running for weeks, consuming electricity reportedly comparable to the entire annual consumption of the state of California (the instructor notes this statistic may be approximate). This energy cost produces significant heat and carbon emissions, creating a tension between AI advancement and sustainability goals. Nvidia and other GPU vendors are actively working on energy-efficient chip architectures in response. The instructor notes that AI can also *help* optimize cloud energy consumption — an important closed-loop opportunity.

- **Site Reliability Engineering (SRE) and DevOps** — SRE professionals command the highest salaries in IT today, the instructor notes, because they are responsible for maintaining 99.999% application availability. They continuously monitor throughput, latency, error rates, and resource health using observability platforms, and implement auto-remediation workflows so that faults are detected and healed automatically before users experience any degradation. DevOps (the integration of developer and operations workflows via CI/CD pipelines) and SRE together ensure that new features can be deployed rapidly and reliably — supporting the "continuous delivery" model that modern digital businesses require.

## Technologies / Frameworks Discussed

| Technology | Type | Key Point |
|---|---|---|
| Microservices Architecture | Application design pattern | Decomposes monoliths into small, independently deployable services communicating via messaging; enables agile development and fault isolation |
| Virtual Machines (VMs) | Server virtualization | Hypervisor divides one physical server into multiple isolated OS instances; raises utilization from 15% to ~40-50% |
| Containers (Docker) | Lightweight virtualization | Share host OS kernel; spin up in seconds; portable across any cloud or edge environment |
| Kubernetes | Container orchestration | Manages container lifecycle, auto-scaling, self-healing, and declarative deployment; ~85% market share |
| Software-Defined Networking (SDN) | Network virtualization | Allows network functions (firewalls, load balancers, bandwidth allocation) to be configured in software |
| Network Function Virtualization (NFV) | Network virtualization | Virtualizes specific network appliance functions, enabling dynamic resource allocation at the network layer |
| DevOps / CI/CD | Software delivery | Continuous integration, continuous delivery, and continuous deployment pipelines that accelerate software release cycles |
| Site Reliability Engineering (SRE) | Operations discipline | Ensures 99.999% availability through monitoring, observability, and auto-remediation |
| Hadoop / Spark / Flink | Big data analytics | Open-source platforms for batch and real-time analytics; run on cloud infrastructure |
| IBM Watson Healthcare | Enterprise AI | Cognitive AI platform processing 3 million pages/minute; used to analyze medical research and reverse-engineer treatment plans |
| Edge AI / TinyAI | On-device intelligence | AI models compressed via pruning, quantization, or knowledge distillation to run inference on resource-constrained edge devices |
| Federated Learning | Distributed ML | Trains models across distributed edge devices without centralizing raw data; enables privacy-preserving edge AI |
| Multicloud / Hybrid Cloud | Cloud deployment strategy | Distributes workloads across multiple cloud providers and private infrastructure to eliminate vendor lock-in |
| Quantum Computing | Next-generation compute | Expected to be fault-tolerant and commercially viable by ~2030; will supersede classical transistor-based computing limits |

## Important Examples and Case Studies

- **Amazon's 1992 Christmas Problem (Cloud Origin Story)** — Amazon's online bookstore experienced catastrophic server overload every December from Christmas shoppers, then watched expensive servers sit idle in January. Rather than absorb the cost, Amazon began renting out spare capacity to external parties — the commercial insight that became AWS and the cloud industry. The instructor uses this to anchor the "on-demand" and "utility computing" concepts that define cloud economics.

- **Flipkart E-Commerce Microservices Decomposition** — Flipkart's monolithic e-commerce platform is being progressively decomposed into independently deployable microservices (shopping cart, payments, analytics, recommendations, etc.). The instructor uses this as a running example: if the payments service needs an upgrade, it can be taken offline and redeployed without touching the shopping cart service. Multiple container instances of each microservice run in parallel; Kubernetes maintains the configured minimum instance count automatically.

- **CAT Exam Server Failure** — A few years ago, approximately one lakh MBA applicants logged into the Common Aptitude Test portal simultaneously. The server administrator had not provisioned for the peak load; the server failed and the examination had to be cancelled. The instructor contrasts this with a cloud-native setup, where auto-scaling would have detected the load spike and provisioned additional containers within seconds — preventing any disruption.

- **Azure Cloud Outage and Boarding Passes** — The instructor references a high-profile incident (believed to be an Azure outage) lasting 11 hours, during which airline check-in systems failed globally. Passengers received their boarding passes on plain white paper. The outage was caused by an administrator misconfiguration, not a hardware failure — underscoring why SRE, auto-remediation, and Mean Time to Recovery (MTTR) metrics are now top-priority investments for cloud providers.

- **Jio Diwali/New Year Load Spikes** — The instructor describes ongoing Jio AI projects that predict seasonal demand surges (Diwali, New Year) and pre-provision memory, storage, and bandwidth for specific microservices accordingly — an example of AI-driven cloud resource management.

- **IBM Watson Healthcare and Cancer Diagnosis** — Watson Healthcare (IBM's cognitive AI platform, requiring 96 CPU cores) ingests a patient's blood report as its only input and cross-references it against all cancer research papers published in the last 50 years — processing 3 million pages per minute. In the cited Tokyo University Hospital case, Watson reversed the medication prescribed by the human oncologist; the patient subsequently recovered. This case is used by the instructor to illustrate the distinction between traditional data analytics (data + programmer-written logic) and AI (data alone — the model supplies its own logic).

- **Jio Edge-Based Access Control System** — When Dr. Raj joined Jio in 2017, door access required a card swipe whose data traveled ~1,000 km to a Navi Mumbai cloud, was authenticated, and a response returned to open the door. If the cloud or internet was down, employees were locked out. The Jio AI team built a Raspberry Pi-sized edge controller deployed at each door — storing all employee records locally — that authenticates and opens the door in nanoseconds with no cloud dependency. The system was later upgraded with a face-recognition AI model (from a California acquisition), eliminating the need for ID cards entirely. The system is now deployed across one lakh Jio office doors and won an internal innovation award.

- **Jio Bangalore Airport CCTV-AI Project** — Jio is developing an AI-enhanced CCTV system for Bangalore airport in which cameras do not merely capture and transmit footage but run on-device AI inference to detect anomalies, suspicious behavior, or security threats in real time — alerting security staff immediately. This is presented as the practical embodiment of Edge AI 2.0.

- **Sarvam AI Cloud Setup** — During Q&A, a student asks about Sarvam AI (an Indian government-backed AI initiative). The instructor confirms Sarvam runs on its own dedicated cloud center (believed to be in Mumbai) and hosts speech recognition and image recognition models accessible for a fee — a model similar to what Jio is also planning to offer, potentially competing with Sarvam in the domestic AI cloud services market.

- **Jio's Email Auto-Response LLM Application** — Jio receives thousands of customer emails daily and employs significant human staff to read and respond. The instructor describes a current project using a multilingual large language model to read and respond to emails within two minutes autonomously — reducing human labor while improving speed. This is presented as a concrete production example of generative AI on cloud.
