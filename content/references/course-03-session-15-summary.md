# Emerging Digital Technologies — Session 15: Group Use-Case Presentations

**Instructor**: [[dr-sumitra-padman|Dr. Sumitra Padman]]
**Date**: Session 15 (approximate date inferred from transcript download: 2026-04-12)
**Course**: Emerging Digital Technologies (Course 03)

Session 15 is a student-led presentation session. Four groups — Group 10, Group 3, Group 1, and Group 4 — each present a 25–35 minute applied use-case study exploring how a bundle of emerging technologies can be integrated to solve a real-world business problem. The session runs for approximately three hours and concludes with Q&A discussions after each presentation. Dr. Sumitra Padman chairs the session, asks probing questions, and offers strategic commentary particularly on the rural education presentation.

The four use-cases span very different industry domains but share a common analytical structure: problem definition, a layered technology architecture, a phased implementation roadmap, risk and ethics analysis, and a financial ROI model. Across all four presentations, several cross-cutting themes emerge: the inadequacy of isolated or siloed technology adoption, the necessity of governance frameworks alongside technical solutions, the shift from one-time transactions to recurring subscription-based revenue models, and the primacy of human-centred design even in highly automated systems.

A secondary theme visible throughout is the tension between ambition and practicality. Every group grapples with the gap between a technically elegant architecture and the messy realities of legacy systems, workforce skill gaps, regulatory environments, and the digital divide. The session is therefore as much a lesson in technology strategy and change management as it is in the individual technologies themselves.

## Key Concepts

- **Blended Intelligent Learning Ecosystem** — Group 10 argues that a training business cannot simply bolt new technologies onto existing content; it must rebuild its entire infrastructure as an interconnected, layered system where each technology reinforces adjacent ones. The seven layers they propose — learner touch points, IoT sensing, immersive UX, cloud content, AI personalisation, API integration, and analytics — are described as mutually dependent rather than optional add-ons. Isolating any one layer weakens the whole.

- **AI-Driven Adaptive Personalisation** — A recurring concept across Groups 10 and 4. AI personalisation engines move beyond "one-size-fits-all" content delivery to dynamically adjust the pace, format, and topic sequence of learning or processing based on real-time behavioural signals. Group 10 gives the example of a learner struggling with a leadership scenario automatically receiving a supplementary microlearning module. Group 4's AP platform envisions a conversational AI interface where the CFO can query cash-flow status in natural language rather than navigating dashboard menus.

- **Microlearning and Just-in-Time Delivery** — Both the corporate training and rural education groups note that shorter attention spans and time-constrained learners make bite-sized, on-demand content modules (10–15 minutes) far more effective than traditional hour-long lectures. VR sessions in particular are flagged as unsuitable for extended periods; the professor herself notes that the literature generally recommends no more than 15 minutes of continuous VR use. Just-in-time delivery is framed as a shift analogous to just-in-time manufacturing: the right knowledge, delivered at the exact moment of need.

- **Immersive Learning via VR/AR and Metaverse** — Virtual and augmented reality appear as core technologies in both the training (Group 10) and rural education (Group 1) use-cases. VR is valued for creating "safe-to-fail" experiential environments — leadership role plays, simulated chemistry labs, field trips — that would otherwise be expensive, dangerous, or geographically inaccessible. The metaverse-enabled smart classroom proposed by Group 10 uses smartwatches, RFID sensors, and AR/VR headsets to simultaneously deliver content and capture engagement data. Group 1 argues VR is a "strategic multiplier" because it replaces multiple costly physical infrastructure items (labs, field trips, specialist teacher visits) with a single shared device ecosystem.

- **IoT-Enabled Smart Environments** — Group 10's architecture includes IoT sensors embedded in VR/AR devices and smart classrooms to monitor physical-behavioural signals: attention patterns, biometric indicators, real-time location, and movement. These signals feed back into the AI analytics layer to trigger adaptive interventions such as pacing changes or suggested breaks. The concept extends the personalisation engine beyond digital behaviour into physical presence.

- **Software-Defined Vehicles (SDV)** — Group 3 presents the automotive industry's transition from hardware-centric to software-centric vehicles as a paradigm shift analogous to the smartphone revolution. Traditional vehicles contain up to 100 distributed Electronic Control Units (ECUs); SDV architecture replaces them with a centralised High Performance Computer (HPC) running a modular software stack. Updates, new features, and even revenue-generating services (heated seats, driver assistance packages) are delivered over-the-air (OTA), turning a one-time hardware purchase into a recurring subscription platform. The SDV market is projected to grow from $56 billion (2025) to $750 billion by 2035, a ~30% CAGR.

- **Open-Source SDV Frameworks and Shared Software Factories** — Group 3 draws an explicit analogy between Android's transformation of the smartphone supply chain and what open-source SDV frameworks (Eclipse SDV, Automotive Grade Linux/AGL) are doing for automotive. Rather than each OEM building a proprietary OS, shared software factories allow all suppliers (Bosch, Continental, Aptiv) and OEMs (BMW, Mercedes-Benz, Volkswagen) to contribute to and consume a common foundation, resolving fragmented supply chains and accelerating time-to-market. DevSecOps pipelines, containerised microservices, and CI/CD automation are central enablers. Companies with strong CI/CD pipelines deploy software 46 times more frequently, per McKinsey research cited by the group.

- **Postcode Lottery and the Rural Education Gap** — Group 1 introduces this UK policy term to frame the global rural education problem: a student's access to quality education is determined by their postal code rather than their ability. The group's literature review finds the same pattern — outdated materials, teacher shortages, poor digital access, underfunding — repeated across rural Midwest USA, Australian outback schools, Scottish highland villages, and Indian rural communities. Infrastructure absence (no reliable electricity or broadband) is identified as a non-negotiable precondition: without it, all emerging-technology interventions remain aspirational rather than operational.

- **VR as a Strategic Multiplier for Rural Education** — Group 1 proposes a phased VR implementation framework that begins with low-cost headsets (e.g., Google Cardboard-class devices) shared among students, with priority investment in teacher training and internet connectivity before scaling. The core argument is economic: a single VR ecosystem simultaneously eliminates the cost of physical science labs, field trips, and specialist teacher visits, making the total ROI positive even for government-funded rural schools. India and Australia are noted as running live pilot programs under their national education plans, targeting production deployment within two to three years.

- **Accounts Payable Automation — From Cost Centre to Intelligence Centre** — Group 4 presents a comprehensive framework to evolve the AP function through four technology generations: legacy desktop automation → OCR/RPA → AI-augmented hyper-automation → agentic AI-driven autonomous processing. The key motivation is that despite 15+ years of automation investment, only 8.8% of organisations achieve OCR accuracy above 90%, and 75% of AP processes remain only partially automated. RPA-only architectures are brittle: they break on multilingual invoices, layout changes due to mergers, or any process divergence. The proposed solution introduces a multi-module architecture with cognitive document processing, AI-powered two-way/three-way PO matching, workflow orchestration, ERP integration, and a conversational analytics layer for the finance team.

- **4Ds Framework for AI Use-Cases** — Group 4 references a framework circulating on social media (attributed to a Netherlands professor) that categorises good AI automation candidates as: **Dangerous** (tasks harmful to humans), **Dull** (repetitive, low-cognitive work like invoice entry), **Dirty** (hazardous-waste-adjacent tasks), and **Difficult** (massive data processing). AP automation falls primarily in "Dull" and "Difficult," making it a textbook fit for AI that relieves human workers of repetitive strain and redirects their effort toward exception management and strategic vendor relationship management.

- **Governance as a Core Architecture Pillar** — All four groups treat governance, ethics, and regulatory compliance not as afterthoughts but as load-bearing components of their architectures. Group 10 highlights algorithmic bias, GDPR, and the psychological impact of immersive environments. Group 3 frames "responsible innovation" and "trust as the north star" for SDV transformation. Group 1 maps six governance pillars across government, schools, tech vendors, infrastructure integrators, teachers/parents, and monitoring bodies. Group 4 covers AI explainability, accountability, fairness, privacy (GDPR/CCPA), regulatory alignment with EU AI Act and OECD frameworks, and mandatory human oversight at critical process decision points.

- **Phased Transformation Roadmaps** — Every group structures its implementation in three to four phases spanning 6–24 months, moving from foundational infrastructure to full ecosystem integration. The logic is consistent: prove value at small scale, build stakeholder trust, develop internal capability, then scale. Group 4 explicitly frames this as tailored recommendations for organisations at different automation maturity levels (0% manual to 65%+ semi-autonomous), arguing there is no single universal starting point.

## Important Examples and Case Studies

**Corporate Training Ecosystem (Group 10 — Shafnaz, Mario, H, Taruna)**
Group 10 proposes a seven-layer technology transformation of a corporate training business (conceptually similar to upGrad-style platforms). Key figures: the global corporate training market is worth $380+ billion and growing at ~9% CAGR, projected to reach $800+ billion within ten years. The ROI model shows stable returns in Year 1 (project planning), a dip in Year 2 (capital investment in VR/AR infrastructure), and a consistent upward trend from Year 3 onwards as the new delivery model generates new client enrollments, ad-hoc training revenue, smart-room rentals, and data monetisation. A notable insight from Q&A: the group had not fully addressed the constraint that humans cannot sustain VR immersion beyond ~15 minutes, which informed their decision to position VR as on-demand just-in-time snippets rather than extended modules.

**Software-Defined Vehicles (Group 3 — Mike, KJ/Tes, Ravi, Sanjay, Ashish)**
The group's anchor analogy is the Nokia/Blackberry disruption by iPhone: automotive OEMs that do not transition to software-defined platforms face the same fate from Tesla, Google, and other ecosystem players. Market data: SDV market projected at $750 billion by 2035 from a $56 billion 2025 baseline. Real-world examples cited include Amazon's Zoox autonomous taxi (being piloted in Las Vegas resorts), Tesla OTA updates saving up to $1,000 per vehicle in recall avoidance, BMW's subscription-based heated seats (generating recurring revenue even on discontinued hardware models), and Bosch projecting $4 billion in annual software revenue from subscription services. Q&A surfaces a sophisticated debate about the Samsung/Motorola/Android analogy — each OEM retains proprietary hardware differentiation (e.g., Volvo's hill-hold feature, BMW's climate subscriptions) while sharing the software OS layer, just as Android phone manufacturers differentiate through screens, cameras, and software skins.

**Rural Education Transformation (Group 1 — Lokesh, Dennis, Jennifer, Pratusha, Bhuvena)**
Group 1's use-case is explicitly ethical in framing, centred on the idea that "location should not be destiny." The group's fishbone/cause-analysis maps how rural education challenges (poor funding, teacher shortages, digital divide, outdated materials) reinforce each other systemically. Their phased framework: Phase 1 — secure foundational infrastructure (electricity, broadband, devices); Phase 2 — low-cost VR pilot with teacher training and community engagement; Phase 3 — scale with cultural localisation, public-private partnerships, and federated learning models. Cost model for a 50-student school: approximately $118,500 all-in for VR headsets, content licensing, teacher training, and support. The "mobile van" concept is proposed as an alternative to per-school fixed installation — a shared, circulating hardware kit reduces per-student cost. India's National Education Plan and Australia's rural VR pilot programs are cited as real-world policy precedents. Dr. Sumitra adds a nuanced counterpoint: for the most underserved rural communities, the challenge is not just technology access but mindset, child-labour economics, and parental education — requiring age-segmented, vocationally-linked learning pathways rather than simply replicating urban curricula digitally.

**Accounts Payable (AP) Automation (Group 4 — Abhishek, Jaya, Norman, Vinai, Sunday)**
Group 4 targets a mid-size organisation processing ~25,000 invoices per year. Baseline metrics: $12 cost per invoice, 15-minute processing time, 4–5% error rate. Target metrics: $4 per invoice (67% reduction), 3-minute processing time (80% reduction), <1% error rate. Projected total savings for a sample organisation: ~$900,000 per year. The group's four-phase phased roadmap — AI co-pilot → semi-autonomous → full autonomy → strategic value creation — maps to organisational maturity levels rather than calendar time, recognising that organisations already at 65% automation maturity cannot go back to Phase 1. The ethical analysis references the EU AI Act's "right to explanation," GDPR, CCPA, OECD AI principles, and the COBIT governance framework. A particularly vivid real-world scenario grounds the problem: a vendor stopping HVAC maintenance services because a three-month-old invoice remained unpaid in a fully manual AP system.
