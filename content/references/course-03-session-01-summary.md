# Emerging Digital Technologies — Session 01: Course Orientation, DBA Journey, and Technology Overview

**Instructor**: [[dr-sumitra-padman|Dr. Sumitra Padman]]
**Date**: Session 01 (first day of coursework, cohort kick-off)
**Course**: Emerging Digital Technologies (Course 03)

Session 01 is a two-and-a-half-hour orientation session led by Dr. Sumitra Padman, who anchors Course 03 and also leads the academic vertical for DBA tech programs at upGrad. Unlike most course sessions, this one carries no technical content delivery. Instead, it serves three purposes simultaneously: introducing the instructor and the rationale behind Course 03, orienting the entire cohort to the three-year DBA journey at Golden Gate University (GGU), and previewing the fourteen sessions and practitioner-expert lineup that will constitute the Emerging Digital Technologies curriculum. The session features an unusually long open Q&A in which learners from highly varied professional backgrounds — finance, cyber security, real estate, software testing, energy, and HR — surface questions about research topic selection, thesis structure, data collection strategy, and how the program accommodates both technical and non-technical participants.

The DBA-specific guidance given in this session is particularly substantive. Dr. Padman walks through the full dissertation lifecycle from coursework to topic proposal to IRB clearance to final defense, emphasizes the importance of starting with a broad research problem on day one, and explains the three submission modes (thesis, book, business plan). She repeatedly frames the courses not as isolated modules but as calibration opportunities — chances for learners to test whether a nascent research idea is technically feasible and appropriately scoped. A central message is that every business professional, regardless of domain, can find a technology-meets-business gap worth investigating, and that the DBA's applied research expectation (a real business implication, not pure theory) is what differentiates it from a PhD.

After the break, Dr. Padman turns to the Course 03 curriculum itself, walking through each technology block, naming the practitioner-expert who will teach it, and explaining how each technology plugs into the AI data pipeline. The framing device is deliberately sequential: data collection (IoT) → data storage and processing (cloud, HPC) → emerging compute paradigms (quantum, neuromorphic) → human-computer interaction (AR/VR) → process intelligence (RPA, cognitive automation) → trust and security (cyber security, blockchain) → simulation and digital representation (digital twins). This sequence mirrors the physical journey data takes from sensor to insight to action.

## Key Concepts

- **Course 03 as the "technology landscape" course** — Emerging Digital Technologies was created specifically in response to learner feedback from earlier cohorts, who found that focusing only on AI was insufficient for their research contexts. The course is designed to give every learner at least one additional technology lens beyond AI/ML. Sessions are application-focused and deliberately not technology-heavy; the goal is pattern recognition — identifying which emerging technology could address which type of business problem — not deep technical mastery. All fourteen sessions are delivered by industry practitioners rather than pure academics.

- **The three pillars of the DBA curriculum** — The entire program is built around three interlocking goals: (1) Strategic application — identifying a real business objective and aligning AI or technology to it; (2) Scalable implementation — assessing whether a proposed solution is viable within a defined organizational or industry scope; and (3) Responsible deployment — accounting for ethical, privacy, governance, and ESG considerations from the outset. Every research project, assignment, and dissertation is expected to demonstrate all three. Dr. Padman stresses that "not every problem needs generative AI" and that selecting the right level of technological complexity is itself a research contribution.

- **DBA vs. PhD — the applied research distinction** — A PhD may produce theoretical knowledge with no immediate application requirement. A DBA at GGU must produce applied research: grounded in seminal theory, but with a demonstrable business or social implication. This shifts the selection criteria for research topics. The work must be researchable, publishable, and actionable. Dr. Padman frequently uses the phrase "a business problem solved with technology" as the canonical DBA research shape.

- **Starting broad and narrowing down** — A recurring piece of advice across multiple Q&A exchanges is to begin with a very broad problem area and resist the temptation to specify a narrow problem statement too early. Narrowing too early risks discovering that someone has already solved that exact problem or that data is unavailable. The literature review process — which GGU's doctoral studies course teaches systematically — is the mechanism for identifying the specific gap that remains unaddressed. Learners are encouraged to use each course's case studies and assignments as informal validation checkpoints for their evolving research question.

- **Research novelty in an era of open-source models** — Several learners raised the concern that with over 1.3 million models available on Hugging Face alone, their research ideas might be pre-empted. Dr. Padman's response is that novelty in a DBA context does not come from building a new model but from applying existing models to a specific business problem in a comprehensive, rigorous, and published way. Open-source contributions are not publications. A complete research contribution requires addressing scalability, ethical dimensions, integration challenges, measurement metrics, and trust — aspects that model authors uploading to GitHub or Hugging Face typically do not address.

- **The dissertation milestone structure** — After completing the six coursework modules and the first immersion (topic presentation), learners enter a 22-month dissertation phase divided into three milestones. Milestone 1 is the Topic Proposal (a qualifying exam, equivalent to two thesis chapters, approved by a faculty committee). Milestone 2 is the Defense of Research Proposal (adding one more chapter). Milestone 3 is the final defense. A thesis supervisor is assigned after the topic proposal is approved. This staged structure means learners are building their final document throughout, rather than writing it all at the end — a deliberate design choice given the two-year constraint.

- **Three dissertation submission modes** — GGU accepts work as a traditional five-chapter thesis, a book (suited for practitioners who want to synthesize broad domain expertise into publishable form), or a business plan (suited for those with market access and implementation ambition). All three formats have the same academic rigor and chapter structure guidelines. Post-defense, a book can be converted to a publishable work by removing the literature review chapter. Patents are recommended for business plans.

- **Research data strategy** — Primary research (surveys, interviews, organizational data collection) requires IRB (Institutional Review Board) ethical clearance before it can begin, which typically happens mid-dissertation. Learners can and should begin with secondary research (published datasets, government data portals, academic literature) immediately. If organizational data is unavailable due to confidentiality, synthetic datasets or published benchmark datasets are accepted for model validation. For highly novel technologies where live testing is impossible, expert-validated frameworks (qualitative validation by domain practitioners) are accepted as a third validation pathway.

- **Publication strategy** — Publishing is strongly recommended but not compulsory. GGU periodically shares conference and journal opportunities. Dr. Padman advises publishing only in reputable peer-reviewed venues to protect intellectual property and get meaningful feedback; publishing in low-quality conferences provides no validation value and exposes research ideas unnecessarily. Conference presentations allow hybrid participation. Assignments done rigorously during coursework can serve as the basis for conference papers — several previous cohort learners have done this.

- **The fourteen-session technology arc** — The course sequences its fourteen sessions as a data pipeline: IoT (2 sessions, data collection) → Cloud Computing (storage and infrastructure) → Quantum Computing and Cyber Security (2 topics, same instructor) → High Performance Computing and Neuromorphic Computing (2 topics, same instructor) → AR/VR Immersive Experiences → RPA and Intelligent Automation (2 sessions) → Blockchain → Digital Twins. This arc is not arbitrary — it reflects the sequence in which data flows from physical world through storage to computation to human interaction to process automation to verification to simulation.

- **Neuromorphic computing as an emerging paradigm** — Dr. Padman gives a brief but clear explanation of neuromorphic computing when asked: it is computing architecture that mimics the structure and energy efficiency of the biological brain, designed for edge deployment (processing at the sensor/device rather than in the cloud). It is particularly relevant to IoT contexts where bandwidth and power are constrained, and to small language model (SLM) deployment. The session notes that this is especially important for organizations that cannot afford enterprise-scale LLM infrastructure — neuromorphic approaches offer a path to AI-at-the-edge for smaller businesses.

- **Cognitive automation as the next stage beyond RPA** — A learner working in intelligent automation flagged that cognitive process automation (CPA) — decision-making automation that goes beyond rule-based RPA to incorporate judgment and context — is displacing traditional RPA in enterprise settings. Dr. Padman acknowledged this as an important gap in the current syllabus and committed to raising it with the RPA session instructor. This exchange illustrates the course's peer-learning philosophy: the cohort's collective practitioner experience actively shapes what gets taught.

## Technologies / Frameworks Discussed

| Technology | Type | Key Point |
|---|---|---|
| Internet of Things (IoT) | Data collection infrastructure | Real-time data acquisition from sensors; foundational input to all AI models; edge and cloud integration |
| Cloud Computing | Storage and distributed processing | Covers public/private/hybrid cloud, virtualization, containerization, microservices; ESG implications of data storage choices |
| Quantum Computing | Emerging compute paradigm | Covers quantum key distribution, cryptography, and use cases where classical computing falls short; not expected to be deeply technical |
| High Performance Computing (HPC) | Compute acceleration | CPU vs. GPU trade-offs; relevant to large-scale AI model training and enterprise analytics |
| Neuromorphic Computing | Brain-inspired edge computing | Mimics biological neural architecture for energy-efficient inference at the edge; relevant to IoT and SLM deployment |
| Augmented Reality / Virtual Reality (AR/VR) | Immersive human-computer interaction | Customer experience, manufacturing simulations, training, data collection in physical environments; integration with AI |
| Robotic Process Automation (RPA) | Intelligent process automation | Rule-based task automation evolving toward cognitive automation; hands-on with UiPath Studio |
| Cognitive Process Automation | Advanced automation | AI-augmented automation with judgment and context; flagged as emerging beyond RPA |
| Cyber Security | Digital trust and threat management | AI-specific security threats; securing digital transformation; relevant especially to finance and critical infrastructure |
| Blockchain | Distributed ledger and smart contracts | Decentralized trust; smart contracts; applications in compliance, supply chain, finance |
| Digital Twin | Simulation and virtual representation | Virtual replica of physical systems; used in manufacturing (Siemens), healthcare, disaster management; validation via expert qualitative review |
| UiPath | RPA platform | Open-source community edition available; hands-on demo planned; task-to-AI-enabled automation workflow |
| Microsoft Azure ML | No-code ML platform | Introduced as a tool for learners without Python coding background to experiment with ML algorithms |
| Google Scholar | Academic research tool | Recommended for initial literature review and scoping prior work on a research topic |

## Important Examples and Case Studies

- **ISRO project via Nirma University** — Dr. Padman describes her own PhD research as an example of applied research: the problem statement came from ISRO (Indian Space Research Organisation), involved space weather parameter prediction using time series and machine learning, and required integrating physics, electronics, and ML. She uses this to illustrate that a DBA research problem can span domains, that the research question itself can come from an institutional source, and that the journey of narrowing a broad topic takes time.

- **Zomato/Swiggy adoption curve** — Used to address the concern of a learner working in real estate whose cross-functional change initiatives were hitting organizational resistance. Dr. Padman cites Swiggy as an example of a technology that nobody anticipated elderly users would adopt, but which they now use routinely. The point: organizational resistance to technology is not a disqualifier for research — it is itself a research gap. The barriers to adoption, the change management challenges, and the governance failures are all publishable research problems.

- **Insurance underwriting with generative AI** — Dr. Padman mentions encountering three or four learners from previous cohorts working on applying generative AI to insurance underwriting problems. Used to illustrate that even domain-specific, seemingly niche applications of AI have enough complexity (data quality, regulatory compliance, trust/explainability) to constitute original research.

- **Finance learner with siloed GenAI tools** — A practitioner example from Dr. Padman's advising conversations: a finance-domain learner identified that generative AI and agentic AI tools had been deployed in silos across their organization, but security integration issues prevented unification. This integration gap — not the AI itself — became the research problem. Used to show that research novelty can come from the organizational and architectural layer, not just the algorithmic layer.

- **Software testing automation with GenAI** — A learner (Jay Lakshmi) described a system she had built that converts software requirements into test cases and then automatically generates Python test scripts using generative AI. The output quality was inconsistent. Dr. Padman reframes this as a publishable research opportunity: improving the reliability and trustworthiness of AI-generated test outputs, creating measurable trust indicators, and benchmarking when the model's outputs can and cannot be trusted.

- **Responsible AI metrics student** — Dr. Padman references a current DBA learner who has developed approximately 20 quantitative measures to assess responsible AI compliance in a specific industry vertical. This is used as a model for how a non-programmer from a business background can produce rigorous, publishable research: by creating measurement frameworks rather than building models.

- **Digital twin in disaster management** — A learner from a manufacturing context raises digital twins in the context of pharmaceutical and textile assembly lines. Dr. Padman notes that Siemens is a benchmark practitioner in this space and that digital twin research ideas that cannot be tested live can be validated via expert practitioner interviews — an accepted qualitative validation methodology in DBA research.

- **Cognitive automation displacing RPA** — A learner with three years of experience in cognitive process automation (CPA) informed the class that cognitive automation is replacing rule-based RPA in enterprise settings. This learner-contributed insight was new information that Dr. Padman committed to incorporating into the session 10 (RPA) agenda. Illustrates the peer-learning dynamic the course is designed around.
